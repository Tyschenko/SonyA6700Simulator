The foundation

Every photo is a balance of three things

These three controls together set how bright your photo is — change one, and you usually need to compensate with another. Each one also has a creative side effect. That side effect is the whole game.

◎

Aperture (f-number)

How wide the lens opening is. Lower f-number = wider opening = more light.

Side effect — depth of field. Low f (f/3.5) blurs the background beautifully. High f (f/11) keeps everything sharp front to back.

⧗

Shutter speed

How long the sensor is exposed. 1/2000s is a blink; 1/30s is slow.

Side effect — motion. Fast shutter freezes action. Slow shutter lets moving things blur (and risks camera shake).

◈

ISO

How much the camera amplifies the signal. Higher ISO = brighter image without changing aperture or shutter.

Side effect — noise. Low ISO (100) is cleanest. High ISO (6400+) brightens dark scenes but adds grain.

    If your photo looks wrong — fix it fast

    Too dark

    Slow the shutter, open the aperture (lower f), or raise ISO. Try ISO first if motion or depth matters.

    Too bright

    Speed up the shutter, close the aperture (higher f), or lower ISO. Lower ISO first to keep it clean.

    Subject blurry

    Shutter too slow for the movement. Raise it to 1/250+ (1/1000+ for sports), then raise ISO to keep brightness.

    Too grainy

    ISO too high. Lower it, then recover brightness with a slower shutter (on a tripod) or wider aperture.

    No background blur

    Use a lower f-number, get closer to your subject, zoom in longer, and move the subject away from the background.

    Not sharp enough

    Stop down to f/8 for landscapes; check focus is on the eyes for portraits; raise shutter to beat camera shake.

    A6700 menu settings worth setting once

    • Auto ISO range: set min 100, max 6400 (push to 12800 only when desperate). Keeps grain in check.
    • Auto ISO min shutter: "Standard" for everyday; "Faster" for wildlife & sports so the camera prioritises freezing motion.
    • Drive mode: Continuous Mid/High for action and wildlife bursts; Single for landscapes.
    • Subject recognition AF: On — set to Human for portraits, Animal/Bird for wildlife.
    • File format: RAW (or HEIF) gives you the most room to fix exposure later while learning.
    • Exposure compensation dial: nudge − for bright snowy/beach scenes, + for dark subjects the meter wants to brighten.
